# "Fool-Proof Kubernetes Dashboards for Sleep-Deprived Oncalls" — Summary

- **00:00 – 02:10** – Tom Wilkie introduces David Kaltschmidt; David sets expectations that the talk won’t hand over turnkey dashboards but will share practices Grafana uses to keep on-call dashboards usable under pressure.
- **02:10 – 04:00** – Barcelona Metro door-handle anecdote illustrates how subtle UX flaws block progress when people are stressed; the same applies to Grafana dashboards when incidents strike.
- **04:00 – 07:10** – Kubernetes troubleshooting is multidimensional and cognitively taxing; naive cloning of dashboards explodes sprawl and tags lose meaning. David proposes a three-level dashboard maturity model: ad-hoc, methodical, and “optimized” (where sprawl is actively pruned and experiences are consistent by design).
- **07:10 – 09:00** – Signs of low maturity: copying dashboards with tags intact, no Git/version control for JSON, and teams browsing folders hoping to find the right view. These behaviors raise on-call toil instead of shrinking it.
- **09:00 – 12:30** – Move toward maturity by embracing template variables (one node dashboard can represent every node/cluster) and by adopting methods such as USE or RED so every new service dashboard follows the same questions (capacity, errors, latency). Grafana’s Kubernetes mixin is cited as an example set of methodical dashboards.
- **12:30 – 15:30** – Build hierarchical (drill-down) dashboards that mirror Kubernetes scopes (cluster → namespace → pod). Each panel should link to the next level so responders can quickly eliminate healthy layers and focus on failing components.
- **15:30 – 18:30** – Split dashboards when magnitudes differ (Cortex ingest vs. query paths) so small but important signals aren’t drowned out. Use expressive visuals—status colors for HTTP codes, normalized y-axes, saturation indicators—to lower the cognitive load when scanning charts.
- **18:30 – 21:30** – Accept that templating can make browsing harder, so lean on alert links that pre-fill variables. Store dashboards as code, enforce reviews/removals of unused ones, and consider meta-analytics to see which dashboards and queries actually get used.
- **21:30 – 24:40** – Achieve “consistency by design” with libraries/high-order functions that generate panels with shared style choices, shrinking diffs and ending debates over visualization options. Reuse community mixins (Prometheus, Consul, etc.) even just for their vetted queries, and envision future workflows where browser edits flow straight into PRs with compiled JSON previews.
- **24:40 – 27:30** – Final takeaways: whatever method you choose must not impede dashboard usability, and don’t become the single-person bottleneck (“persona metric”) for dashboards. Q&A touches on wrangling 1,800+ dashboards in a multi-team Grafana: internal training plus evangelism help teams understand how their dashboards affect everyone else.
